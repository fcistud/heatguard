How it works

The science, explained

Each concept below is explained twice — a plain-language version, then the technical detail. The whole pipeline turns one weather reading into one safe instruction for a crew.

ℹ️ The standards stack

HeatGuard is built on ISO 7243 (WBGT), ISO 7933 (Predicted Heat Strain), ISO 8996 (metabolic rate), the ACGIH TLV / Action-Limit screening tables, and NIOSH acclimatization guidance. Each section below maps to one of these.

WBGT — the heat-stress index #

Plain Air temperature alone is a poor measure of danger. WBGT (Wet-Bulb Globe Temperature) is the standard index that also accounts for humidity, sun, and wind — the things that decide whether your body can actually cool itself. A humid, sunny 38 °C can be far more dangerous than a dry, breezy 45 °C.
Technical WBGT (ISO 7243) is a weighted blend of three thermometers: 
WBGT = 0.7·T_nwb + 0.2·T_g + 0.1·T_db      (outdoor, with sun)
WBGT = 0.7·T_nwb + 0.3·T_db                  (shade / no sun)
  • T_nwb (natural wet-bulb) captures evaporative cooling — how well sweat can shed heat. Its 0.7 weight is why humidity dominates: in humid air sweat can't evaporate, so the body can't cool.
  • T_g (black-globe) captures radiant load — direct sun.
  • T_db is plain air temperature.

HeatGuard estimates outdoor WBGT from weather (air temp, humidity, wind, solar radiation, and the sun's position) using the validated Liljegren (2008) model via ECMWF's thermofeel, with a Stull natural-wet-bulb fallback at night. Or it takes a measured reading from an on-site meter, which bypasses estimation. Every value carries its provenance (liljegren | fallback | measured). The black-globe temperature for the radiant load is recovered consistently from the WBGT value and fed to the PHS model as mean-radiant temperature — never T_r = T_db, which would badly understate strain under sun.

The work-rest cycle — ACGIH / ISO 7243 #

Plain Given how hot it is (WBGT), how hard the job is, and whether the worker is acclimatized, published safety tables say what fraction of each hour is safe to work — 100%, 75%, 50%, 25%, or STOP. Hotter or harder → more rest.
Technical HeatGuard hardcodes the ACGIH TLV (acclimatized) and Action-Limit (unacclimatized, ~3 °C stricter) screening tables. A step lookup by metabolic category returns the highest work allocation whose WBGT ceiling ≥ the current WBGT; above the most permissive ceiling → STOP.

The hard-coded TLV (acclimatized) table — WBGT °C ceilings:

The Action Limit (unacclimatized) table is ~3 °C stricter throughout (e.g. moderate 100% → 25.0). The mapping is a step lookup, not interpolation — the standard's intent and what an inspector expects: scan 100 → 75 → 50 → 25 and take the first allocation whose ceiling ≥ current WBGT; above the 25% ceiling → STOP. A "—" cell means that allocation has no screening WBGT for that intensity (the physiological PHS layer catches metabolic danger there). A separate continuous risk_score is exposed for the UI gauge only — the legal cycle stays stepped.

Hydration and max exposure — ISO 7933 PHS #

Plain A physiological model simulates the worker's heat balance to predict how much they'll sweat (so HeatGuard can tell them how much water to drink) and how long they can keep going before overheating.
Technical HeatGuard runs the ISO 7933:2023 Predicted Heat Strain (PHS) model via pythermalcomfort. Given air temperature, radiant temperature, wind, humidity, metabolic rate, clothing, and the worker's weight/height/acclimatization, PHS integrates the heat balance over time and outputs core temperature, sweat loss, and the time to reach physiological limits.

Two calls per decision, by design:

  • a 60-minute call at the cycle-weighted metabolic rate → sweat loss (the per-hour water target; 1 g ≈ 1 mL) and end-of-hour core temperature;
  • a 480-minute call at the full working rate → the true minutes-to-limit (the max safe continuous exposure), which becomes a work-fraction cap.
⚠️ Validity envelope and clamps

PHS is only valid for metabolic rate M ∈ [100, 450] W/m² and radiant temp T_r ≤ 60 °C (and T_db ≤ 50, v ≤ 3), so inputs are clamped to that envelope — Gulf desert globe temperatures can exceed it (a black-globe can hit ~63 °C, which silently NaNs the whole result). The d_lim_* outputs and sweat_loss_g are cumulative over duration, which is exactly why the two-call (60 min / 480 min) design is required.

Acclimatization — the NIOSH ramp #

Plain A body adapts to heat over ~1–2 weeks. A brand-new arrival is far more vulnerable — and is the group that actually dies. So new workers should ramp up exposure gradually.
Technical Per NIOSH guidance, a brand-new worker is capped at 20% of normal exposure on day 0, rising ~20%/day to 100% by day 5 ([0.20, 0.40, 0.60, 0.80, 1.00]); a heat-experienced-but-new-to-the-job worker ramps over ~2 days ([0.50, 1.00]). While ramping, the worker is also screened against the stricter Action-Limit table.

The cap only binds when heat stress is actually present — a newcomer still works normally in a cool dawn. This is pure scheduling — zero cost — targeting the deadliest group.

The decision — most-conservative-of-three #

The called work fraction is the minimum of three independent limits:

eff_fraction = min( ACGIH_table_fraction ,        # regulatory (ISO 7243)
                    acclimatization_cap ,         # new-worker protection (NIOSH)
                    PHS_cap = max_safe_min / 60 ) # physiological (ISO 7933)
  1. the ACGIH / ISO 7243 table (regulatory),
  2. the acclimatization cap (new-worker protection, only when heat stress is present), and
  3. the PHS physiological cap (max safe minutes ÷ 60).

STOP fires only when no safe block remains (work under ~5 minutes) or WBGT is above the table's most-permissive ceiling. Otherwise a work-rest cycle is prescribed, and one signal is broadcast. The advisory carries the signal, the cycle, the hydration target, the acclimatization fraction, the WBGT plus provenance, a continuous risk score, and a human-readable rationale. live_signal() expands an advisory into the minute-by-minute broadcast (Work block → Drink pulses → Rest break).

PHS can only tighten the schedule, never loosen it

Because the called fraction is the most conservative of the three, the regulatory table is always the backstop. If the physiological model can't solve even after clamping, HeatGuard falls back to a conservative estimate — the schedule can only get stricter, never more permissive.

The compliance log — a tamper-evident record #

Every reading, cycle, drink prompt, water attestation, and STOP is appended to a SHA-256 hash-chained log: each record commits to the previous one (record_hash = sha256(prev_hash + canonical_json(body))), so any later edit or deletion breaks verify_chain(). It exports to CSV (the audit binder) and JSONL (directly verifiable).

It is:

  • worker-protective first — the worker's own immutable heat-safety history;
  • a compliance shield second — verifiable proof against fines and liability; and
  • privacy by design — it records site conditions and protective actions, not individual location, biometrics, or tracking. The only identifier is a worker/crew id the operator controls and can pseudonymise.

Impact, ROI, and scale #

The impact model is mechanistic: it credits AKI cases averted in proportion to the dangerous hours HeatGuard catches that the calendar ban misses, anchored to the Nicaragua effect sizes (AKI −94%, productivity +10–20%). The ROI monetises this conservatively (headline = productivity + recovered safe work + AKI averted + fines avoided; death and turnover excluded). The scale projection extrapolates the per-crew result to a workforce → danger-hours, AKI cases, lives saved, and value.
